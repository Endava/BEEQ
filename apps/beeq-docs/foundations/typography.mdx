## Typography defines hierarchy

Typography plays a crucial role in defining hierarchy within design. Variable weights convey different levels of importance, where bolder weights indicate greater significance. Good type positioning also establishes visual prominence and emphasizes key information.

## Line height

Line height for text styles is important to ensure legibility. Determining the correct line height can differ between different typefaces and sizes, so it's important to adjust and experiment until it looks and "feels" right — it's not an exact science.

## Line length

Set the reading environment to suit the reader. Shorter lines are usually more comfortable to the reader. As the line length increases, the user's eye has to travel further from the end of one line to the beginning of the next, making it harder to track their progress vertically. Aim for an average line length of 50–90 characters, including spaces.

### Display

As the largest text on the screen, display styles are reserved for short, important text or numerals. They work best on large screens. Can be used for content-based layouts such as news articles or content-heavy pages and larger marketing page layouts where larger fonts are needed.

<Frame caption="Display text style">

  />

### Heading

Headings are best suited for short, high-emphasis text on smaller screens. These styles can be good for marking primary passages of text or important regions of content.

Heading styles come in a range of sizes and can be used in a range of contexts, such as:

- building page hierarchy,
- helping users scan large chunks of text,
- providing a title to a page or piece of content,
- as subheadings or eyebrow headings, where there is still only one H1 tag per page (as per accessibility requirements).

    When considering capitalization in text, it's crucial to think about its impact on readability, visual consistency, and the tone of the text. Capitalization helps guide the reader's eye and improve understanding, so it should be used consistently to best suit the content and users.

    **Do** Use sentence case (capitalizing the first letter of the first word) for titles, headings, menu items, labels, and buttons. Capitalize proper nouns and product names. Maintain consistency in your capitalization style throughout your UI.

    **Don't** Avoid using all caps for long sentences or paragraphs, as it can be hard to read and may appear as "shouting". Refrain from mixing different capitalization styles within the same context.

    Properly formatting lists is essential because it enhances readability, simplifies information, and leads users through the interface in a clear, logical way. List items should follow the body text type and BEEQ tokens to ensure the correct font, color, size, and spacing are applied.

    **Do** Maintain consistency in font, color, size, and alignment across all list items. Use indents for sub-items. Employ bulleted lists for unordered items and numbered lists for sequential or ordered items.

    **Don't** Avoid mixing bulleted and numbered lists within the same section. Maintain consistent capitalization styles. Refrain from including too many items in one list — break long lists into subcategories if necessary.

    <Frame caption="Lists">
      <img

  <Card title="Text alignment">
    Text alignment determines how text is positioned within a page, column, or grid, and it plays a significant role in readability and visual consistency. We advise against using indentation.

    **Do** Align text to the left for languages that read left to right, as it's the most natural and easy for the eye to follow. Use centered alignment for short texts like headings, captions, or decorative texts.

    **Don't** Avoid center alignment for paragraphs longer than two lines. Refrain from using justified alignment without proper hyphenation settings. Avoid mixing different alignments in the same paragraph or section. Do not use right-aligned text except for RTL languages.

    <Frame caption="Text alignment">

    Truncating text can significantly affect its legibility and understanding. When text is cut off, important information may be lost, leading to confusion or misinterpretation. To enhance accessibility and user experience, avoid truncation unless absolutely necessary.

    **Do** Use truncation for long lists of items (e.g. breadcrumb components) or when text exceeds available space. Employ truncation for text spanning two or more lines with no user input required.

    **Don't** Avoid truncation in text for labels or titles. Refrain from truncating or wrapping text in components intended for short UI labels like buttons or lozenges — revise content to use fewer words instead.

## Accessibility

Typography holds immense importance in guaranteeing accessibility and readability for users with diverse abilities and requirements. In UI/UX design and design systems, it's vital to account for the following aspects to enhance typography accessibility:

1. **Clear and legible fonts:** Opt for fonts that are clear and legible, particularly for users with dyslexia, low vision, or reading challenges.
2. **Responsive typography:** Develop a typography system that is responsive and adaptable to different devices and screen sizes, ensuring accessibility and readability across various platforms.
